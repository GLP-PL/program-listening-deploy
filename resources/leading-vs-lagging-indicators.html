Leading vs lagging indicators

The difference between spotting a problem early and explaining a failure after the fact often comes down to which signals you are watching.

4 min readDelivery Intelligence

Two types of signals

Every organisation tracks metrics. But not all metrics are created equal. The fundamental distinction is between lagging indicators - which tell you what has already happened - and leading indicators - which signal what is about to happen.

Most organisations are drowning in lagging indicators while starving for leading ones. This imbalance explains why so many problems seem to appear suddenly, when in reality they have been building for weeks or months.

Lagging indicators: the rear-view mirror

Lagging indicators are the metrics that appear in status reports, board packs, and quarterly reviews:

  • Milestone completion dates
  • Budget burn rates
  • Defect counts
  • Customer complaints
  • Staff turnover

These metrics are valuable for accountability and learning. But by the time they move, the damage is done. You cannot un-miss a milestone or un-lose a key team member.

Leading indicators: the early warning system

Leading indicators are patterns that precede problems - signals that something is shifting before it shows up in formal metrics:

Decision velocity

When decisions start taking longer, or getting revisited more often, it often signals emerging uncertainty or misalignment.

Communication patterns

Changes in how teams communicate - more escalations, fewer cross-team conversations, increased formality - can indicate growing strain.

Effort-to-output ratio

When teams are working harder but producing less, it suggests hidden friction or technical debt accumulating.

Informal workarounds

The rise of unofficial processes and shortcuts often precedes formal process breakdowns.

Why organisations struggle with leading indicators

Leading indicators are harder to track for several reasons:

  • They are often qualitative rather than quantitative
  • They require continuous observation, not periodic measurement
  • They are context-dependent and hard to standardise
  • They rely on pattern recognition across multiple data sources

Shifting the balance

The goal is not to abandon lagging indicators - they remain essential for accountability and learning. The goal is to complement them with leading indicators that provide early warning.
