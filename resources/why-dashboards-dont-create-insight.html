Why dashboards do not create insight

Dashboards show data. Insight requires context, pattern recognition, and forward-looking interpretation that most dashboards cannot provide.

6 min readDelivery Intelligence

The dashboard paradox

Organisations have never had more dashboards. Project dashboards, executive dashboards, team dashboards, real-time dashboards. Yet leaders consistently report feeling less informed, not more.

The problem is not a lack of data. It is that dashboards present data without the context needed to interpret it. They show what is happening without explaining why it matters or what to do about it.

Data is not insight

There is a crucial distinction between data and insight:

Data

Raw numbers and facts

Insight

Understanding that enables action

A dashboard can tell you that a metric has changed. It cannot tell you whether that change is significant, what caused it, or what you should do in response.

The limitations of dashboards

Snapshot thinking

Dashboards show the current state, but insight often comes from understanding trajectories and patterns over time.

Missing context

A red indicator means different things in different contexts. Dashboards cannot capture the nuance that determines whether something is a crisis or a minor blip.

Interpretation burden

Every dashboard requires someone to interpret it. That interpretation takes time, expertise, and attention - all of which are scarce.

Gaming and distortion

When metrics become targets, teams optimise for the dashboard rather than the underlying outcome. The numbers look good while reality deteriorates.

What insight actually requires

Real insight requires more than data visualisation:

  • Pattern recognition across multiple data sources
  • Understanding of organisational context and history
  • Forward-looking interpretation of current signals
  • Translation into actionable recommendations

Beyond the dashboard

The solution is not better dashboards. It is a different approach to generating insight - one that does the interpretive work rather than leaving it to already-overwhelmed leaders.
