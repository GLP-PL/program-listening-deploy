What is delivery drift?

Delivery drift is the gradual, often invisible, movement away from intended processes, standards, and outcomes. Understanding it is the first step to preventing it.

5 min readDelivery Intelligence

The invisible problem

Every organisation experiences drift. It happens slowly, almost imperceptibly, as small deviations from planned processes accumulate over time. A shortcut here, a workaround there, a decision delayed by a day - individually, these changes seem harmless. Collectively, they can fundamentally alter how work gets done.

Delivery drift is particularly dangerous because it often goes unnoticed until something breaks. By the time problems surface - missed deadlines, budget overruns, quality issues - the underlying causes have been building for weeks or months.

How drift manifests

Drift shows up in many forms across an organisation:

Process drift

Established procedures get modified informally. Teams develop their own ways of working that diverge from documented standards.

Scope drift

Requirements expand gradually without corresponding adjustments to timelines or resources.

Priority drift

What was once urgent becomes routine. Strategic initiatives get deprioritised in favour of operational firefighting.

Communication drift

Information flows break down. Teams operate in silos. Decisions get made without consulting affected parties.

Why traditional monitoring fails

Most organisations rely on lagging indicators to track delivery health - status reports, milestone completions, budget burn rates. These metrics tell you what has happened, not what is happening or what is about to happen.

By the time a milestone is missed, the underlying drift has been occurring for some time. Traditional reporting systems are designed to measure outcomes, not to detect the subtle patterns that precede problems.

The cost of undetected drift

When drift goes unnoticed, organisations pay in multiple ways:

  • Reactive interventions cost more than proactive adjustments
  • Trust erodes when surprises become common
  • Teams become defensive rather than collaborative
  • Strategic objectives get sacrificed for short-term fixes

Detecting drift early

The key to managing drift is visibility - seeing the early warning signs before they become visible problems. This requires continuous observation of patterns that indicate emerging strain.
